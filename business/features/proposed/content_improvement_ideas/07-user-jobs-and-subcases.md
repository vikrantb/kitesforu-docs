# User Jobs and Subcases

This file is the concrete "user perspective" layer.

It answers:

- what the user is actually trying to achieve
- what emotional state they are often in
- what a strong human coach, teacher, editor, trainer, or storyteller would realize about that situation
- what kinds of content patterns are therefore worth exploring

The goal is to stop thinking in broad content buckets and start thinking in user jobs.

## 1. Interview Prep

### What the user is trying to do

The user is rarely trying to "consume interview content."

They are trying to:

- stop blanking under pressure
- sound sharp without sounding rehearsed
- translate messy experience into clean proof
- adapt to the company, role, and round
- recover confidence after weak interviews

### Common subcases

- first-time candidate with weak story inventory
- experienced candidate who rambles
- career switcher who struggles to translate background
- strong candidate who freezes under pressure
- final-round candidate who needs polish, not more theory
- senior candidate who needs judgment, trade-offs, and leadership proof
- technical candidate who can solve problems but struggles to narrate thinking

### What a strong human coach sees

- many candidates do not lack experience; they lack retrieval structure
- the hardest part is often selecting and shaping proof in the moment
- users need contrastive hearing: weak answer vs credible answer
- confidence comes from pattern recognition and repetition, not generic encouragement

### Content implications

The best ideas here will likely help users:

- build a reusable story bank
- recognize what a question is really testing
- hear exactly why an answer sounds weak, vague, or brittle
- rehearse under realistic pressure, not ideal conditions

## 2. Exam Prep

### What the user is trying to do

The user is trying to:

- pass a high-stakes exam
- reduce panic and mental noise
- identify what is high-yield
- stop making the same mistake repeatedly
- move from recognition to real recall under time pressure

### Common subcases

- behind and overwhelmed learner in rescue mode
- competent learner who keeps missing trap questions
- learner who knows concepts but cannot retrieve quickly enough
- advanced learner aiming for top-band performance
- certification learner who needs realism and decision speed

### What a strong tutor sees

- learners often misdiagnose their own problem
- they mistake familiarity for mastery
- what they need most is usually not more explanation, but better sequencing:
  - retrieval
  - feedback
  - discrimination
  - revisit

### Content implications

The best ideas here will likely:

- expose misconceptions early
- force recall before explanation
- build pressure tolerance
- distinguish rescue mode from mastery mode

## 3. Skill Mastery

### What the user is trying to do

The user is trying to:

- become fluent enough to actually perform
- stop feeling clumsy or fragmented
- make fewer mistakes in real-world execution
- connect theory to practice

### Common subcases

- novice who does not yet have a mental model
- learner who can imitate but not generalize
- learner stuck at intermediate plateau
- hobbyist who wants enjoyable progress
- professional who needs reliable execution

### What a strong teacher or coach sees

- users need a clear move set, not just topic coverage
- performance improves when feedback is attached to moments of action
- examples must be paired with gradual independence, not dumped all at once

### Content implications

The best ideas here will likely:

- teach a few reusable moves extremely well
- pair worked examples with live correction logic
- rotate between technique, error correction, and application

## 4. Teaching, Explainers, and Lecture Support

### What the user is trying to do

The user is trying to:

- understand something confusing without getting lost
- prepare to teach or present clearly
- connect abstract ideas to concrete situations
- remember the point of a lecture, not just its parts

### Common subcases

- student who needs plain-language orientation first
- advanced learner who wants depth and nuance
- teacher who wants a memorable way to explain something
- listener who wants a clean big-picture synthesis

### What a strong teacher sees

- learners get lost when they cannot locate the main question
- examples do more work than definitions
- a good explainer often wins by making one thing click deeply, not by covering everything

### Content implications

The best ideas here will likely:

- open with a question, puzzle, or misconception
- use example families
- end with transfer and teach-back, not just summary

## 5. Explainer Podcasts

### What the user is trying to do

The user is trying to:

- understand a topic while staying engaged
- feel guided through complexity
- retain the main line without constant rewinding
- enjoy the experience enough to keep going

### Common subcases

- business listener who wants strategic clarity
- science listener who wants mechanisms made legible
- history listener who wants causality and stakes
- health listener who wants practical relevance without oversimplification
- true-crime listener who wants narrative tension and evidence logic
- technology listener who wants implications, not jargon theater

### What a strong audio producer sees

- audio cannot carry the same density as text unless it is heavily structured
- the listener must know where they are in the argument
- vivid moments and signposting matter disproportionately

### Content implications

The best ideas here will likely:

- organize around one driving question
- alternate scene, explanation, and synthesis
- use recurring signposts so the listener never loses the thread

## 6. Corporate Training and Team Communication

### What the user is trying to do

The user is trying to:

- handle a real work situation better
- avoid a mistake
- train a team without boring them
- understand what changed and what to do next
- coach managers and employees toward consistent behavior

### Common subcases

- compliance or risk topic that is usually dead on arrival
- first-time manager who needs judgment and scripts
- sales or enablement content that must be applied tomorrow
- onboarding content that must build confidence over time
- internal team update that must be skim-friendly but actionable

### What a strong workplace learning designer sees

- employees care about realism, relevance, and next-day usefulness
- learning transfer often fails without manager reinforcement
- "what changed / why it matters / what to do now" is a powerful communication backbone

### Content implications

The best ideas here will likely:

- begin with a realistic decision moment
- include follow-on manager reinforcement
- teach gray-zone judgment, not just official rules

## 7. Storytelling

### What the user is trying to do

The user is trying to:

- feel something specific
- be carried through an emotional arc
- get novelty without confusion
- experience a distinctive genre promise that actually lands

### Common subcases

- horror listener seeking dread and residue
- mystery listener seeking curiosity, clue logic, and fair surprise
- romance listener seeking chemistry, yearning, and earned payoff
- comedy listener seeking rhythm, escalation, and callback reward
- bedtime listener seeking calm, safety, ritual, and descent
- sci-fi listener seeking wonder plus human stakes
- drama listener seeking emotional complexity and social tension

### What a strong storyteller sees

- each genre is an emotional contract, not just a plot wrapper
- users notice immediately when the tone is wrong for the promise
- strong genre content depends on pacing, reveal order, and texture, not just premise

### Content implications

The best ideas here will likely:

- be built around genre-specific arc logic
- use recurring motifs and rule systems
- preserve a strong ending feeling, not just an ending event
