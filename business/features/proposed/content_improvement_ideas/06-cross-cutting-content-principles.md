# Cross-Cutting Content Principles

These principles cut across all content types, but they should be applied differently in each one.

## 1. Content should be built around the user's actual job

Every content piece should answer:

- what is the user trying to do?
- what would success look like?
- what emotional or practical state are they in?

This is why the same content structure should not be used everywhere.

## 2. Each content type should have its own progression logic

Examples:

- interview prep: diagnose -> build stories -> rehearse -> stress test -> refine
- exam prep: retrieve -> explain -> practice -> revisit -> discriminate
- horror: unsettle -> dread -> reveal -> escalate -> consequence
- bedtime: wonder -> comfort -> soften -> settle -> close
- workplace training: scenario -> choice -> consequence -> reinforcement

The progression is the content. Not just the words.

## 3. Good content alternates information with action

Action can mean:

- recall
- choice
- reflection
- prediction
- scene change
- emotional turn

Pure explanation quickly becomes thin, even if it is well written.

## 4. Quality often comes from contrast

People learn and feel more clearly when content shows:

- wrong vs better
- calm vs pressure
- before vs after
- safe vs dangerous
- superficial vs specific

Contrast is often more powerful than extra explanation.

## 5. Repetition should be purposeful, not lazy

Strong content often repeats:

- a motif
- a structure
- a question type
- a phrase
- a story pattern

But the repetition should reinforce recognition, safety, or mastery, not create monotony.

## 6. The ending matters disproportionately

In many categories, the ending determines whether the piece feels successful:

- bedtime: must settle
- horror: must leave residue
- interview prep: must leave a clear takeaway
- exam prep: must leave a retrievable summary or next action
- workplace comms: must leave the reader knowing what changed and what to do

## 7. Content should reveal editorial judgment

Users can feel when content has a point of view.
That usually improves trust.

Editorial judgment can show up as:

- what to include
- what to omit
- what to sequence first
- what to emphasize

Genericity is often just the absence of visible judgment.

## 8. Strong content should often feel like a guide, not a dump

The best content in the product will often feel like:

- "let me show you how to think about this"
- "here is the pattern"
- "watch out for this trap"
- "here is the move that matters"

That is very different from:

- "here is a lot of information on the topic"

## 9. Richness is not the same as density

Dense content can still be shallow.
Rich content usually has:

- a clear point of view
- the right examples
- the right sequencing
- the right emotional or cognitive pacing

## 10. Future content decisions should ask one question first

Before creating or upgrading a content type, ask:

> If a great human coach, teacher, editor, storyteller, or trainer were making this, what would they do that a generic generator would not?

That is the standard this folder is meant to sharpen.
