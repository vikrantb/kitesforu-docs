# Concrete Idea Bank

This file turns the research into candidate ideas that can later be:

- rated
- challenged
- merged
- prioritized

## Suggested rating dimensions for later review

When someone evaluates these ideas later, a useful scorecard is:

- user pain intensity
- frequency of the user job
- distinctiveness vs generic AI output
- fit for concise audio / podcast output
- breadth of variation
- emotional payoff
- trust-building potential

## 1. Interview Prep

### I1. Story Pressure Cooker

**What it is**

Take one real experience and force it through 5 different interview framings:

- leadership
- conflict
- failure
- prioritization
- influence without authority

**Why it matters**

Harvard-style story-bank prep says candidates need flexible stories, not one memorized answer.

**Example**

A product launch story gets retold as:

- "Tell me about a time you led through ambiguity"
- "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a stakeholder"
- "Tell me about a time you failed"

**Why it feels human**

A real coach often says: "You already have the story. The problem is you don’t yet know how to bend it."

### I2. Weak Answer / Better Answer Autopsy

**What it is**

Play a weak answer, then a stronger version, then explain the differences:

- vague vs precise
- activity vs impact
- polished-sounding vs believable
- overlong vs tight

**Example**

"I collaborated with cross-functional teams" becomes
"I pulled design and analytics into a single daily decision loop, cut contradictory asks, and got the release back on schedule by Friday."

**Why it feels human**

Users can hear the difference instead of being told generic advice.

### I3. Skeptical Follow-Up Drills

**What it is**

After the user hears or gives an answer, hit it with realistic follow-ups:

- "Why was that actually hard?"
- "What did you personally do?"
- "How did you know that worked?"
- "What would your manager say you should have done differently?"

**Why it matters**

This is where brittle prep usually collapses.

### I4. Company Lens Rewrites

**What it is**

Take one candidate story and reinterpret it through different company lenses.

**Example**

The same story is rewritten to foreground:

- ownership and trade-offs
- collaboration and clarity
- customer obsession
- hypothesis-led thinking

**Why it feels human**

It resembles how a good coach helps candidates translate the same truth into different evaluator lenses.

### I5. Freeze Recovery Capsules

**What it is**

Short content for the moment when the user blanks:

- how to buy time gracefully
- how to restate the question
- how to choose a story quickly
- how to recover after losing the thread

**Why it matters**

Many users’ real problem is not knowledge but performance collapse.

## 2. Exam Prep and Skill Mastery

### E1. Retrieval Sandwich

**What it is**

Every unit follows:

1. try from memory
2. hear the explanation
3. retrieve again in a slightly different form

**Why it matters**

This directly maps to retrieval-practice evidence and keeps content from becoming passive review.

### E2. Why This Wrong Answer Was Tempting

**What it is**

A dedicated format for analyzing attractive wrong answers.

**Example**

Instead of only saying why choice C is correct, explain:

- why B felt plausible
- what clue would have ruled B out
- what pattern to watch next time

**Why it feels human**

Great tutors spend a lot of time inside learner confusion, not just correct explanation.

### E3. Worked Example Ladder

**What it is**

For difficult problems:

- fully worked example
- partially worked example
- learner attempt
- correction

**Why it matters**

Edutopia and broader worked-example research show novices need ambiguity reduced before independence increases.

### E4. Rescue Mode Triage

**What it is**

A high-pressure mode for users who are behind:

- top high-yield ideas
- top recurring mistakes
- top "if you only fix three things, fix these"

**Example**

For a bar learner:

- issue spotting misses
- rule precision gaps
- conclusion without analysis

### E5. Mastery Spiral

**What it is**

A longer-horizon pattern that revisits ideas with increasing difficulty and more transfer.

**Why it matters**

Advanced learners need widening application, not just more facts.

## 3. Teaching, Explainers, and Lecture Support

### T1. Misconception Cold Open

**What it is**

Start by naming the wrong intuition that many learners hold.

**Example**

"A lot of people think inflation always means every price just rises together. That is not what makes inflation hard."

**Why it feels human**

Good teachers often begin exactly where students are likely to be wrong.

### T2. Example Family Trio

**What it is**

Teach each key idea through:

- obvious case
- borderline case
- false friend

**Why it matters**

This helps learners build discrimination instead of shallow recognition.

### T3. Lecture Companion Staircase

**What it is**

A companion structure that gives:

- what the lecture is really about
- one worked example
- one self-check
- one transfer question

**Why it matters**

Many learners need re-orientation more than more lecture.

### T4. Teach-Back Minutes

**What it is**

Short moments where the learner must explain the idea in plain language or to a novice.

**Why it feels human**

This converts passive understanding into active structure.

## 4. Explainer Podcasts

### P1. One Big Question Episodes

**What it is**

Each episode is anchored to one question the listener genuinely wants answered.

**Example**

- "Why do smart companies still get phished?"
- "Why are some recessions inflationary and others deflationary?"
- "What actually makes placebo effects real?"

### P2. Scene -> Mechanism -> Consequence

**What it is**

Open with a concrete moment, then explain the system, then return to what it changes.

**Why it feels human**

This creates both narrative pull and conceptual clarity.

### P3. Competing Explanations Format

**What it is**

Present two or three plausible explanations, then sort which survives the evidence.

**Why it matters**

This keeps the listener engaged in judgment, not just receiving conclusions.

### P4. Signpost-Rich Audio

**What it is**

Use explicit listener orientation:

- "Here’s the idea to hold onto"
- "Now the mechanism"
- "Now the twist"
- "Here’s why this matters"

**Why it matters**

Transom-style audio craft suggests listeners need stronger guidance than readers.

## 5. Corporate Training and Team Communication

### C1. Decision Fork Scenarios

**What it is**

Start with a realistic moment where the employee must choose:

- ignore or escalate
- say it now or say it later
- shortcut or follow the process
- assume or verify

**Why it matters**

ATD-style scenario learning works because it makes judgment visible.

### C2. Near-Miss Storylines

**What it is**

Train around stories that almost became incidents.

**Example**

- almost-clicked phishing email
- unclear consent handling
- new manager conversation that almost damaged trust

**Why it feels human**

Near misses are memorable and concrete without being lecture-ish.

### C3. Manager Huddle Packs

**What it is**

Companion content for managers:

- one debrief question
- one behavior to look for
- one follow-up reminder a week later

**Why it matters**

CIPD research repeatedly points toward manager support as a major transfer lever.

### C4. Day 1 / Week 1 / Month 1 Journeys

**What it is**

Instead of one onboarding blob, create staged content arcs.

**Why it feels human**

It matches the reality of how confidence and relevance change over time.

### C5. What Changed / Why It Matters / What To Do Now

**What it is**

A default structure for internal comms and briefings.

**Why it matters**

It reduces the common failure mode where updates are skimmed but not acted on.

## 6. Storytelling

### S1. Horror Dread Ladder

**What it is**

Escalate through:

- wrongness
- disturbance
- evidence
- proximity
- irreversible consequence

**Why it matters**

Writer’s Digest and horror craft guidance repeatedly return to dread, pacing, and controlled escalation.

### S2. Mystery Fair-Play Chain

**What it is**

Build mystery around:

- visible clue
- plausible red herring
- hidden motive pressure
- reveal that feels surprising but earned

**Why it feels human**

The listener should feel they could have solved it, even if they didn’t.

### S3. Romance Intimacy Ladder

**What it is**

Stage closeness through:

- spark
- resistance
- vulnerable reveal
- setback
- earned emotional payoff

### S4. Comedy Premise Engine

**What it is**

Instead of random funny lines, create one comic engine that keeps producing escalation and callbacks.

**Example**

A teacher who keeps trying to seem ultra-competent while every minor classroom technology choice humiliates them in increasingly public ways.

### S5. Bedtime Descent Arc

**What it is**

Move from:

- gentle curiosity
- soft exploration
- comfort
- slowing
- closure

**Why it matters**

Sleep Foundation guidance points toward low-arousal endings, routine, and consistency.

## 7. Missing but likely valuable hybrid ideas

### H1. Interview + Job Search Confidence Companion

Not just interviews, but:

- translating experience
- clarifying strengths
- handling rejection
- preparing for difficult networking or recruiter calls

### H2. Exam + Motivation Rescue Tracks

Content that addresses:

- shame spirals
- avoidance
- panic before sitting down to study
- how to restart after bad mock scores

### H3. Teacher Content That Helps Explain Better, Not Just Learn Better

This is distinct from lecture support. It is about:

- finding better analogies
- sequencing explanations
- anticipating confusion
